Offer in Compromise  ·  IRC Section 7122(f)

The IRS Has 24 Months to Reject Your Offer in Compromise — Or It's Legally Accepted

This rule has existed since 2005. For most of that time, it was theoretical — the IRS almost always responded in time. In 2026, with over 26,000 IRS employees gone and OIC cases sitting untouched for months, it has practical significance for the first time.

⚖️ IRC Section 7122(f) — The Deemed Acceptance Rule

The rule: Under IRC Section 7122(f), if the IRS does not accept, reject, or return your Offer in Compromise within 24 months of receiving it, the offer is automatically deemed accepted by law — at whatever amount you offered. You owe that amount, not the full original tax debt. The IRS confirms this directly on its OIC page: "Your offer is automatically accepted if the IRS doesn't make a determination within two years of the IRS receipt date." Time in appeals is excluded from the count.

Why this rule matters more right now than ever before

26,000+ IRS employees who left in 2025 under DOGE reductions
28% Staff reduction in IRS Appeals alone
6 mo+ Some OIC cases sitting untouched with no assigned officer

The IRS has always moved slowly on OICs — 12 to 18 months is normal processing time. But the agency has historically managed to act within the 24-month window. That calculus has shifted.

Yoav Betsion, an Enrolled Agent who has handled IRS remediation for 20 years and works closely with Romeo Razi, described what he's seeing directly:

Yoav Betsion, EA — IRS Remediation Specialist

"We have cases where documents sat for seven months without anyone opening them. A nice lady on the phone told us: 'Here is the truth. Nobody opened even the file.' We send things to the service center and they just sit there for months. The clients think we're not doing anything — but the truth is the IRS doesn't even open them."

He also described a specific collection appeals case:

Yoav Betsion, EA — On an active case

"We went to appeals and even when you go to appeal for collection, they still have to review your financials. They don't do it. That part goes back to regular collection. When collection reviews the financial, they send it back to appeals. We had a case where we waited 6 months for collection to review the financials. Nothing happened. I found in the IRS manual that if collection doesn't reply to appeals within 45 days, appeals has the right to analyze the financials themselves. So we forced the appeals officer to close the case. But what happened now — they're overworked with cases. Usually in appeals they're supposed to have 50 cases per officer. Right now they have 80 to 90."

The IRS's Independent Office of Appeals lost approximately 28% of its staff in 2025. The former Appeals chief, who resigned in September 2025, told Bloomberg Tax that remaining employees were "under tremendous pressure to close cases quickly" — which means some cases are being rushed while others are simply sitting. An OIC case that doesn't get assigned, or gets assigned to an officer who then leaves, can drift toward 24 months without anyone acting on it.

What the law says exactly

IRC Section 7122(f), added in 2005 by the Tax Increase Prevention and Reconciliation Act, states:

"Any offer-in-compromise submitted under this section shall be deemed to be accepted by the Secretary if such offer is not rejected by the Secretary before the date which is 24 months after the date of the submission of such offer. For purposes of the preceding sentence, any period during which any tax liability which is the subject of such offer-in-compromise is in dispute in any judicial proceeding shall not be taken into account in determining the expiration of the 24-month period."

— IRC Section 7122(f)

Congress added this provision specifically to hold the IRS accountable for timely processing. Before it existed, the IRS could sit on an OIC indefinitely. The 24-month deadline was the fix.

Confirmed by the IRS itself

On IRS.gov's official Offer in Compromise page: "Your offer is automatically accepted if the IRS doesn't make a determination within two years of the IRS receipt date. (This does not include any appeal period.)"

The case that confirmed this rule is real and enforceable

For years, practitioners knew the rule existed but had no court case testing it. That changed in 2022 with Brown v. Commissioner, Tax Court case 11519-20L.

The taxpayer in that case owed over $50 million. He submitted an Offer in Compromise and argued — when the IRS eventually rejected it — that the rejection came too late and his offer should be deemed accepted under Section 7122(f). The Tax Court took up the case and specifically addressed two important questions:

  • Does the Tax Court have jurisdiction to hear a deemed-acceptance claim? Yes, the Tax Court confirmed it does — through the Collection Due Process review process.
  • Does time in Appeals count toward the 24 months? The IRS argued that the clock stopped during Appeals review. The court addressed this directly, with the IRS contending that the offer was rejected when collection returned it to the taxpayer — before it reached Appeals.

The case confirmed that deemed-acceptance claims are live, litigable, and that the Tax Court will hear them. The rule is not theoretical. It has been tested in federal court.

How the 24-month clock works in practice

1
Day 0 — IRS receipt date The clock starts the day the IRS physically receives your Form 656 and Form 433-A/B — not the day you mailed it. Keep your certified mail receipt as proof of mailing, and confirm the IRS receipt date in writing.
2
Weeks 1–4 — Initial review IRS checks whether the offer is processable. If it's incomplete or non-compliant, they return it. A returned offer restarts the clock if you resubmit.

If they can process it, they send you a letter with an estimated contact date. The 24-month clock is running from Day 0.

3
Months 1–18 (typical) — Investigation The assigned officer reviews your income, expenses, asset equity, and ability to pay. They calculate your Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP). Normal processing takes 6–18 months for most cases. During the 2025–26 period, this phase is running longer due to staffing reductions and cases going unassigned.

Key risk period: If your case is assigned to an officer who then leaves under the DOGE reductions, it may sit reassigned or unassigned while the clock continues to run.

!
Month 18–24 — Approaching the deadline If you haven't heard back and your case is approaching 18–20 months, your practitioner should be monitoring the status actively. Track your certified mail receipt date. Keep copies of all correspondence.

The IRS is not going to proactively tell you your deadline is approaching. You have to know when it is.

!
Month 24 — Deemed acceptance if no determination If the IRS has not accepted, rejected, or returned your offer by this date, the offer is automatically deemed accepted under IRC Section 7122(f).

Important: Time during which your case is in Appeals is excluded from the 24-month count. The clock stops during judicial proceedings over the underlying tax liability.

✓
After deemed acceptance — your obligations You owe whatever amount you offered. Pay according to the terms you specified (lump sum or periodic payment). File all tax returns on time for 5 years. Pay all taxes owed for 5 years.

Failure to comply with post-acceptance requirements lets the IRS revoke the acceptance and reinstate the full original liability.

How a $2.1 million debt was settled for $205,000 — and what the IRS missed

Yoav Betsion described a real case that illustrates how OIC timing and the formula work in practice. It didn't trigger deemed acceptance, but it shows how long the process can take and where the IRS's own delays can create outcomes practitioners use:

Real OIC case — Yoav Betsion, EA

"We had a client a few years ago who owed 2.1 million to the IRS. We offered 75,000 out of 2.1 million. Between the time you submit the offer and until they make a decision is about a year and a half. I told the client: do not change how much money you're making. He did. They came back with a counter offer of 205,000. We settled."

"One thing I always say about offering compromise: we never negotiate. The only thing we call negotiating is interpretation — of the IRS manual, of what's exempt and what's not. The IRS's formula has very little room. You work within it."

"And this is very important to say: once you settle, you have to be in compliance for 5 years. File and pay on time, every year. If you miss, the offer is canceled and you owe all the back taxes — including the 1.8 million they forgave."

This case also illustrates the variable the IRS often uses against taxpayers: the counter-offer. In most OIC cases, the IRS doesn't simply accept or reject. It calculates its own RCP figure and comes back with a counter. The taxpayer's income changing during processing — which this client did — is the most common reason OICs end up at a higher number than the original offer.

The obligation that trips people up after acceptance

Whether your OIC is accepted normally or triggered through the 24-month deemed-acceptance rule, the post-acceptance requirements are identical — and violating them is the single most common reason people lose OIC benefits after winning them.

Post-acceptance requirements — all 5 years

File all required federal tax returns on time. Pay all taxes owed on time. Make all required estimated tax payments for the current year. If you miss any of these for 5 years after acceptance, the IRS can declare the OIC in default — and demand the original full amount, less whatever you've already paid.

This is not theoretical. The IRS monitors post-acceptance compliance and does declare OICs in default. If you worked for two years to settle $400,000 down to $40,000, then missed a $3,000 estimated tax payment in year three, you can find yourself owing $400,000 again. This is the cliff that most OIC success stories don't mention.

What the staffing situation means for OIC filers in 2026

The IRS lost approximately 26,000 employees in 2025 — roughly a quarter of its workforce — and its OIC unit has not been immune. Here's what this means practically for someone submitting or monitoring an OIC right now:

  • Document your receipt date with precision. Send your offer by certified mail with return receipt. Keep the tracking number and delivery confirmation. The deemed-acceptance clock starts on IRS receipt, not mailing — but having proof of mailing protects you if the IRS claims they never received it.
  • Follow up at the 6-month mark. Call the OIC phone line (1-800-829-1040) at 6 months if you haven't received a contact letter. Ask for the status of your case and whether it has been assigned.
  • Track the 24-month anniversary date. Mark it in your calendar the day you receive confirmation your offer was accepted for processing. Know what date the clock runs to.
  • Do not change your financial situation during the review. The IRS can recalculate your RCP at any point during the 24 months using updated financial data. If your income increases, your required offer amount increases.
  • Don't file an OIC as a delay tactic. The IRS is aware that the 24-month rule creates a potential incentive to file frivolous offers near the end of the Collection Statute Expiration Date. Cases filed close to the CSED for reasons that appear primarily tactical can be returned without starting the clock.
The IRS is not your timer

The IRS will not notify you when you're approaching the 24-month mark. Nobody will call to warn you. You have to know when your deadline is — and if you have a practitioner, so do they.

Frequently asked questions — OIC deemed acceptance

What does it mean for an Offer in Compromise to be deemed accepted?
Under IRC Section 7122(f), if the IRS does not accept, reject, or return your OIC within 24 months of receiving it, the offer is automatically deemed accepted by law. You owe whatever amount you offered — not the original full tax debt. The IRS confirms this on its website: "Your offer is automatically accepted if the IRS doesn't make a determination within two years of the IRS receipt date."
How long does the IRS have to respond to an Offer in Compromise?
24 months from the IRS receipt date. Time during which the underlying tax liability is in judicial proceedings is excluded. After 24 months without a determination, the offer is legally deemed accepted.
Has an OIC ever actually been deemed accepted under the 2-year rule?
The 2022 Tax Court case Brown v. Commissioner confirmed that deemed-acceptance claims are live and litigable. The Tax Court has jurisdiction to hear them through the Collection Due Process process. The rule is not just theoretical — courts will enforce it.
Does the 2-year clock stop during IRS appeals?
Yes. Time during which the underlying tax liability is in dispute in any judicial proceeding is excluded from the 24-month count. The clock also doesn't run during any appeal period after a determination is made. This means total elapsed time can exceed 24 months without triggering deemed acceptance if a significant portion was spent in formal proceedings.
What are my obligations after an OIC is accepted (or deemed accepted)?
Pay the agreed offer amount per your submitted terms. File all required federal tax returns on time for 5 years. Pay all federal taxes owed on time for 5 years. Make required estimated tax payments for the current year. Failing any of these in the 5-year window can result in the IRS declaring the OIC in default and demanding the original full tax liability minus payments already made.
Should I file an OIC now, given the IRS staffing situation?
OIC eligibility is determined by your financial situation — specifically your Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP) — not by IRS staffing. You should file an OIC if you genuinely cannot pay your full tax liability. The current staffing environment makes it more likely that processing takes longer, which means monitoring the 24-month window is more important than usual. Consult a tax professional to evaluate whether your numbers actually support an OIC before filing — filing an offer you don't qualify for wastes time and potentially application fees.
